THE NORTH AMERICAN MONSOON

A seasonal transformation
of the desert Southwest.

Each summer, the intense heating of the North American continent shifts the subtropical high-pressure ridge northward. This allows deep tropical moisture—primarily surging up the Gulf of California—to invade the desert Southwest. The result is a dramatic change from the hot, bone-dry early summer to frequent afternoon and evening thunderstorms.

The North American Monsoon (also called the Southwest Monsoon) delivers more than half of the region's annual precipitation in just a few months. It is not a gentle rain. It is violent, localized convection capable of producing flash floods, massive haboobs, and powerful microbursts that rival the strength of many hurricanes' winds.

Moisture Source
Gulf of California "surges" are the dominant low-level moisture transport mechanism.
Diurnal Cycle
Storms typically fire over mountains in the afternoon and move onto the lower deserts by evening.
High Stakes
Critical for water supply, wildfire season end, and agriculture—but also deadly when underestimated.

THE FORECASTER'S DEFINITION

How do we know the monsoon has started?

LEGACY OPERATIONAL RULE

Tucson 54°F Dewpoint Rule

The monsoon onset at Tucson is declared on the first day of the first three consecutive days when the daily average surface dewpoint temperature at KTUS reaches or exceeds 54°F.

This threshold, adjusted for Tucson's higher elevation, has long served as a reliable operational marker for the arrival of sufficient low-level moisture to support organized thunderstorms across southern Arizona.

This legacy criterion remains one of the most referenced benchmarks among forecasters for tracking the true meteorological start of the North American Monsoon in the Tucson area.
MODERN CONTEXT

Since 2008, the NWS uses a fixed June 15 – September 30 "monsoon season" for products and messaging. However, the actual meteorological onset—the first significant Gulf surge and thunderstorm activity—still varies dramatically year to year, typically occurring between late June and mid-July in southern Arizona.

Why predicting onset matters
  • Fire weather planning and resource allocation
  • Flash flood preparedness in urban and desert areas
  • Understanding interannual variability and climate signals
  • Testing real-world forecast skill at subseasonal timescales
